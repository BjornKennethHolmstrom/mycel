Claude:
Mycelium is genuinely one of the richest biomimicry sources for distributed systems because it solved the same problems — no center, self-repair, resource routing, resilience — through hundreds of millions of years of selection pressure.

Some concrete architectural parallels worth exploring:

**Hyphal growth toward gradients.** Mycelium doesn't map territory uniformly, it grows *toward* resources and pulls back from depleted areas. In network terms: nodes could strengthen connections that carry frequent meaningful traffic and let rarely-used paths quietly atrophy. Adaptive topology rather than static routing.

**Anastomosis.** When two hyphal threads meet they can fuse, creating redundant loops. The network literally heals itself by forming new connections around damage. This is already a p2p concept but mycelium does it *locally* without any coordination signal — pure emergent repair.

**Two speeds of signaling.** Fungi have slow chemical gradients (resource availability, long-range) and faster electrical signals (alarm, immediate response). A Mycel network could mirror this — a slow ambient layer for presence and resource awareness, a faster layer for active communication.

**Spore dispersal.** Long-distance colonization through lightweight packages that contain everything needed to bootstrap a new node. Beautifully analogous to how someone in a new community could seed a local mesh with minimal infrastructure.

**The wood wide web dynamic.** Trees share surplus resources through the mycelium with trees in deficit — not through a market, through a gradient. That's basically the Hearts currency logic you already have, but with a biological existence proof.

The deeper appeal here is that mycelium isn't just a metaphor — it's proof that this *architecture actually works* at planetary scale without coordination overhead. That's a strong foundational story for the project.
