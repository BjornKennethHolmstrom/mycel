## What Mycel Offers That Frameworks Can't

### 1. A path that doesn't require permission

The frameworks often require treaties, government cooperation, global consensus. Mycel requires only that two people install it and find it useful. It grows along gradients of need, not approval. This is how all resilient systems grow: from the ground up, through real relationships, not from the top down through institutional fiat.

### 2. Offline-first resilience

The frameworks assume stable internet, reliable power, functional governance. Mycel works when the internet doesn't. It falls back to Bluetooth and local mesh. This is not a nice-to-have. It is essential for crisis resilience and for the communities most in need of coordination infrastructure.

### 3. Organic trust topology

The frameworks assume governance bodies, certification processes, standards boards. Mycel builds trust through interaction history. A connection strengthens when real exchange happens and atrophies when it doesn't. No moderation team, no algorithm, no central authority. Trust is emergent, not imposed.

### 4. Two-speed signaling

The frameworks are deliberative, slow, designed for governance processes that take months or years. Mycel has both slow (ambient presence, resource gradients) and fast (encrypted coordination, crisis signals). It can handle a community garden planning meeting *and* a storm response. The frameworks describe the long arc; Mycel handles the immediate.

### 5. Temporary constellations

The frameworks create institutions that are designed to persist. Mycel creates temporary coordination groups that dissolve when they're done. A neighborhood response to a flood forms, coordinates, disperses. The infrastructure remains; the group is gone. This matches how humans actually coordinate: in bursts, around specific needs, with people we trust.

## What Mycel Needs from the Frameworks (and Vice Versa)

### Mycel needs from the frameworks:
- **Vocabulary**: What is the minimal shared tag vocabulary for offers, needs, capacity, mood? The frameworks have been defining these categories for years. Mycel needs a small, extensible set that is useful without being prescriptive.
- **Event kinds**: Mycel uses Nostr event kinds for presence, offers, needs, Hearts, constellations. The frameworks could help define these in a way that aligns with broader GGF data standards.
- **Place-based identity model**: Should nodes represent households, workshops, or individuals? This has significant governance implications. The frameworks have thought about BAZ-level identity; Mycel needs a compatible model.

### The frameworks need from Mycel:
- **A path to implementation**: Without Mycel, the frameworks remain documents. With Mycel, they become tools.
- **Organic scaling**: The frameworks describe BAZs as governance units. Mycel provides the infrastructure for BAZs to emerge organically, not be declared.
- **Resilience**: The frameworks assume stability. Mycel assumes instability and works anyway. This is essential for crisis response and for the communities most in need.
